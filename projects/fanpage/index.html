Pikachu

1996

Pokémon Red and Blue

As the Game Boy only supported 2-bit colors and 8-bit graphics, Pikachu was displayed in black and white with pixel graphics in the original Pokémon Red and Blue games. Pikachu was chosen as the primary mascot of the game in an attempt to appeal to female viewers and their mothers because it was believed that Pikachu presented the image of a recognizable pet to children.

1999

Pokémon Gold and Silver

Pokémon Gold and Silver were the first of the Pokémon games to be introduced in color with the development of the Game Boy Color, which featured a color screen with 15-bit RGB color graphics. Pikachu's color was finalized to be yellow, as yellow is a primary color and easier for children to recognize from a distance.

2002

Pokémon Ruby and Sapphire

In 2002, Pokémon came to the Game Boy Advance in full color and made significant strides toward enhancing game mechanics and technology that were not possible in previous versions. While still limited to 15-bit RGB color graphics, greater resolution allowed for a full-color, more detailed Pikachu with red circular pouches on their cheeks, which can spark with electricity.

2006

Pokémon Diamond and Pearl

Pokémon Diamond and Pearl were the first games released on the Nintendo DS, which supported 8 times as many colors as the Game Boy Advance or Game Boy Color. Pikachu now appeared more 3D-like, with the ability to support different shades of yellows and browns allowing to create shading and shadows on the form. Greater resolution also supported more complex movement.

2014

Pokémon Omega Ruby and Alpha Sapphire

Pokémon Omega Ruby and Alpha Sapphire were the first versions to reveal 3D graphics with the development of the Nintendo 3DS, a massive change to the franchise’s video game graphic design. Pikachu is now fully 3D and is designed with multiple camera angles and perspectives in mind. A single frame on the 3DS main display is 400 x 240 pixels compared to a single frame from the DS being 256 x 192 pixels. As a result, Pikachu is designed with smoother lines and curves.

2016

Pokémon Go

Taking the world by storm, by blurring the line between reality and the digital world, Pokémon Go introduced the most realistic Pikachu design yet in order to create a highly immersive virtual experience using augmented reality. There are no visible pixels--housed on mobile devices, Pikachu is displayed in higher frame rates, allowing for smoother animations.
